Philosophy of this website

I have made this website with certain values in mind. I want it to be a part of the free internet, I don't want to make any money from it and I don't want to collect any data.

The Free Internet

Tim Berners-Lee, widely credited as being one of the creators of the modern-day internet, famously made the decision to release the World-Wide Web to the public domain for free. He did that in 1993, literally one week before I was born. In doing so, he ensured that it would be a decentralised platform which requires no licenses or fees to use or build upon.

This decision led to the internet being an incredible place in the 90s. People were making little websites, finding niche communities, and humanity was connecting with each other. Early-days internet is the inspiration for this website! It's a place for me, some dude, to share all this shit with whoever finds my URL.

The internet has changed a lot since the 90s. More and more people use it, and it's now an integral part to all of our lives. As more eyes are cast onto it, so is the shadow of capitalism. At some point, companies realised they can start making money on the internet. Online payments became a thing, and shortly after that, the behemoth that is digital advertising. In around 2016, the algorithmic timeline on social media became mainstream, and since then we've been witness what people called 'the enshittification of the Internet'. With the onset of advertising, paywalls, dark design practices, addictive content and more, the Internet as we know it has become actively worse to the benefit of the corporations who influence it the most.

Now we are at the point where people rely on the Internet and are forced to interact with the unbelievably shitty online world of today. It has gotten so bad that we have forgotten what the Free Internet is all about. It's about people putting things on there for free. It's not about making money. It's about community, and it's about fun! With this website, I will not make any money. It's there as a little corner of the Internet, publicly available, and I hope a little source of joy for anyone who finds it.

Data Collection

Websites which are run by companies want to make money by a) showing you adverts from third parties, or b) selling your data to third parties so they can advertise to you in other ways. This is bullshit and antithetical to the concept of the Free Internet.

I pledge to not collect any data at all! Fuck that!

But Fraser, I hear you cry, you can use my data for good and simply not sell it. Yes, you're right, but you don't know me. As long as that data exists, it can be sold. What if I'm hacked? For me, the only solution is to not store it in the first place.

But Fraser, I hear you cry, I love your newsletter and I don't want to figure out how to listen to your RSS feed, can't I just give you my email address and you send me an email when you publish a newsletter? Many of you have asked me this, but I don't want to collect your email addresses. They could be sold to advertisers! Also, it's just shit that I have to look after. I'll have these real-life email addresses in my database and I don't want to deal with people telling me to unsubscribe them and stuff.

Looking around for alternatives, I learned about RSS and it really is perfect! I update my RSS feed with a newsletter, and anyone who is listening to the feed gets a notification. I don't have to collect your email address, you the reader have the power over how you receive the newsletter. Also, did you know that loads of content online is already available as RSS feeds? It's still widely used today. It's standardised versions of any kind of content, and when you subscribe to a feed, you get that content without having to visit the website. You don't have to subject yourself to ads or giving your email address. I recommend to anyone reading this to get into it, it's a nice step towards consuming content in a more controlled way.

Another topic people have asked me is whether I can see the IP addresses of people visiting my website, and the answer is yes. I use Nginx which is a super cool technology which acts as a reverse proxy, redirecting all the internet traffic to my angular index.html and my assets. Nginx automatically logs that traffic, but I just straight up delete it. What am I gonna do with this information? I briefly considered setting up a 'You're the 1000000th visitor' pop up, because that would be fun, but it's not worth it. I feel like the person who gets it would think it was fake. Also, I wouldn't be surprised if most of the traffic on this site is actually just bots. I have such an insane amount of text on here that those hungry LLMs will be lapping it up, whether or not I have a pathetic robots.txt protecting me.

Cookies

I was asked if I need to have a cookie banner, and no I don't need that! Cookies are used for analytics and tracking data. If you see websites with cookie banners, that means that they are using your data to make money.

What I do sporadically use is local storage - this is a way for my website to tell your local browser some up-to-date info. For example, when you're listening to music on the daily soundtrack archive, I'll plop the information which song you're currently listening to into your browser's local storage so that if you refresh the page, you will still be on the same song.

This is entirely different to cookies! By doing this, I am not harvesting any data from you, I am simply giving you information for you to save locally on your browser.

Use of AI

Since a couple of people asked about AI, I wanted to address it here. In general, I feel like generative AI is a super bad thing and it's gonna cause a million problems for the world. I personally don't like consuming AI-generated anything, so naturally none of the content on this website is AI-generated. I have written all the words myself and have poured myself as a human being into it.

I have, however used it a bit for some of the pages. I started this website originally to a) put my best video games of the year online after I quit twitter, and b) to practice using Angular, the frontend we use at work. So setting up this whole thing and getting it running using Nginx, Cloudflare and my Raspberry Pi was entirely a Fraser-only endeavour.

At work we've started using some AI for bugfixes and stuff, and I decided to try out vibe-coding some projects on this site. Also, since Stack Overflow isn't really a thing anymore, I've used AI to answer coding questions relating to Angular a lot. I used AI to help with the music players and filters on the Daily Soundtracks and Smash soundtrack pages - they were super complicated, and I knew absolutely nothing about streaming audio. I also used it on the contact page to make a cute little game. I used AI on the Mario Kart page to set up the system where you can flick between the different games. I've also used AI to restructure some of the Angular components for me, and to more generally help me with CSS, because I don't have an eye for design at all. Finally the AI has written some unit tests for some of the more complicated parts of the site.
